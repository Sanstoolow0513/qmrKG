\abstracten

The systematic organization of course knowledge holds significant value for teaching and research in computer networking. Large Language Models have emerged as powerful tools for automated knowledge extraction, yet the annotation of domain-specific educational materials remains challenging due to specialized terminology and complex conceptual relationships. Manual construction of knowledge graphs suffers from low efficiency, high cost, and scalability limitations. Automated knowledge graph construction from unstructured course documents addresses these challenges and provides a foundation for intelligent educational applications.

A seven-stage pipeline was designed and implemented to transform unstructured PDF documents into structured knowledge graphs. Document preprocessing employs multimodal vision language models for optical character recognition, converting textbooks, lecture notes, and examination papers into structured Markdown text. A recursive header-aware text chunking algorithm was developed to address long document processing challenges, combining hierarchical heading awareness with token budget control to achieve semantically coherent text segmentation. The knowledge extraction stage adopts a two-phase paradigm with Chinese prompts. First, four entity types are identified from course texts: protocol, concept, mechanism, and metric. Then four semantic relation types are extracted: contains, depends\_on, compared\_with, and applied\_to. Two extraction modes, zero-shot and few-shot, were implemented through prompt engineering. An evidence verification gating mechanism ensures triple traceability. To resolve entity ambiguity, an embedding-based entity canonicalization method was proposed, utilizing FAISS indexing for accelerated similarity retrieval and the Union-Find algorithm for entity identity determination and merging. Upon completion of knowledge fusion, data is batch-imported into Neo4j graph database and visualized through an interactive frontend built with the Next.js framework, supporting force-directed graph layout for relationship navigation and query exploration.

Experiments were conducted on a corpus of 205 computer networking course documents, yielding 16,438 text chunks totaling approximately 10.4 million tokens. The resulting knowledge graph contains 23,600 entities (16,719 concepts, 3,063 mechanisms, 2,189 protocols, and 1,629 metrics) and 44,526 relational triples (15,251 contains, 14,291 applied\_to, 9,601 depends\_on, and 5,383 compared\_with). Entity frequency ranges from 1 to 2,657 occurrences, with the most frequent entities being TCP (2,657), UDP (1,411), IP (1,020), and HTTP (932). The pipeline demonstrates effective automated knowledge graph construction for networking education, providing a computable knowledge infrastructure with clear structure and comprehensive coverage.
